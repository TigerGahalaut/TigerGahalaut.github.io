The COVID-19 pandemic was an event that reverberated through every facet of society, and the city of Chicago was no exception. The changes brought about by COVID influenced every aspect of urban life, especially crime. We can see from the trend line below that the lockdowns that occurred in 2020 and 2021 caused the crime rate to significantly decrease. This was to be expected. However, the circumstances of COVID has also led to unexpected surges in other types of criminal activity. For example, violent crimes like homicide and arson saw a marked increase. The economic hardship brought about by the pandemic led to increased stress and desperation among many individuals, which in turn fueled violent behavior. Job losses, financial instability, and the strain of prolonged isolation created a volatile environment. This scatterplot visualizes the relationship between crime rates and median household income across different community areas in Chicago. Notice a general negative correlation between income and crime rate, indicating that more affluent communities tend to experience lower crime. The impact of COVID-19 on these trends is significant, as the pandemic exacerbated socioeconomic disparities. Lower-income communities, which already faced higher crime rates, experienced further strain due to economic hardships, job losses, and limited access to resources during the lockdown periods. Meanwhile, more affluent communities were better equipped to manage the challenges brought by the pandemic, resulting in less dramatic changes in crime rates. The Loop is an outlier among the high-income communities. This is because it is a major commercial and cultural hub, attracting a large number of people daily, including tourists, workers, and residents. The high density and constant flow of people create more opportunities for crimes like theft, robbery, and assault. Furthermore, the Loop is home to many high-end stores, banks, and businesses, making it an attractive target for crimes like burglary, shoplifting, and financial fraud.

We can clearly see that virtually every community of Chicago experienced a drop in crime during COVID, followed by an increase in crime as the pandemic subsided. We can also see the stark disparities in crime rates across different parts of the city. For example, the South Side of Chicago is known to be one of the most dangerous places areas of Chicago, and notice how it consistently experiences higher crime rates compared to other areas. In contrast, the North Side, which includes many of the city's more affluent neighborhoods, exhibits lower crime rates. The fact that the South Side of Chicago is also where the poorer, more marginalized communities have historically been shows how the geographical distribution of crime in Chicago is deeply rooted in the city's history of segregation and economic disparity. During the pandemic, these disparities became even more pronounced as lower-income communities, primarily in the South, faced greater economic hardship and limited access to resources. During the pandemic, with the implementation of lockdowns and social distancing measures, there was a noticeable shift in the locations where crimes occurred. Public spaces, which traditionally saw high crime rates, experienced a decline as fewer people were out and about. Conversely, residential areas saw an increase in crime rates, as more people were confined to their homes. This shift in crime locations underscores how the pandemic altered daily routines and social behaviors, thereby influencing where and how crimes were committed.
